Helian
A dementia care logbook built from a pivot

Context

Every source pointed to the same problem: families don't recognize dementia as a disease. Doctors agreed. Nursing home staff agreed. We had expert consensus and a working education prototype.

Then we went to an Alzheimer's Indonesia caregiver support meeting. The caregivers weren't asking for education. They already knew what dementia was. The real problem was that critical, constantly changing care data lived in one person's head with no way to share it. 2 of 3 elderly patients with disabilities receive full-time homecare from family members.

We killed the education app and started over. The brief: build a tool that lets a new caregiver pick up a patient's care plan and immediately know what to do for the next 2 hours.

I owned product direction, research synthesis, and end-to-end UX design. Worked directly with Alzheimer's Indonesia and tested with 3-4 real caregivers managing real patients. See the process walkthrough below.

Process and reflection
Replace: Research slide — expert consensus vs caregiver reality. Assumed problem (awareness) vs actual problem (information transfer). Quote from caregiver support meeting.
Replace: Direction decision slide — 3 options considered: messaging app (killed), shared calendar (killed), dedicated care logbook (shipped). One sentence rationale for each.
Replace: Insight slide — "Center the patient's care plan, not the caregiver's schedule." How this framing decision shaped every feature that followed.
Replace: Design decisions slide — 3 key patterns: 15-minute activity stacking, Cautions always visible, structured categories with free-text notes. One sentence rationale per pattern.
Replace: Usability testing slide — 3-4 real caregivers. Primary scenario: "A new caregiver picks up the app and figures out what to do for the next 2 hours." Key findings.
Replace: Outcomes slide — 10+ media features, 1 of 2 apps highlighted by Alzheimer's Indonesia, UX Collective publication. Quote: "One of the best ideas for an app I have ever seen." (Retired home care nurse, British Columbia)
