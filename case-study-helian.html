The pivot
What we assumed
Awareness is the biggest challenge
Every report pointed to the same thing: families don't recognize dementia as a disease — they think it's just aging. We validated this with doctors and at nursing homes. Everyone nodded along. Then we went to an Alzheimer's caregiver support meeting and the whole thing fell apart.
What we found
Caregivers were drowning in logistics
The caregivers weren't asking for education. They already knew what dementia was — they were living with it every day. The real problem: critical, personalized, constantly changing care data trapped in one person's memory with no reliable way to share it.

Decisions
01
Direction
Why a logbook — not a chat app or calendar
We considered three directions. Messaging would bury care info between grocery lists. Calendars center the caregiver's schedule — we needed to center the patient's care plan.
Killed
Messaging app
Caregivers already use WhatsApp. Care information gets buried between casual messages, grocery lists, and family group chat noise.
Why killed: You can't reliably search "what medication did Dad take at 2pm last Tuesday?" in a chat history.
Killed
Shared calendar
Calendars add cognitive overhead to people who are already overwhelmed.
Why killed: A calendar centers the caregiver's schedule. We needed something that centers the patient's care plan.
Shipped
Shareable care logbook
A dedicated space where all care information lives in one place — activities, medication schedules, mood changes, medical cautions. Structured enough that a new caregiver can pick it up and immediately know what to do. Shareable so the whole care team sees the same truth.
"A dedicated app means one more thing to install and maintain. But the alternative — stuffing care data into tools designed for something else — was exactly what was already failing."
Design rationale
02
Design
Small decisions that prevent daily crises
For dementia patients, the small contextual details are what prevent daily crises. A new caregiver walking in doesn't just need to know what to give — they need the behavioral context that changes how care is delivered.
Shipped
15-minute activity stacking
Activities grouped into 15-minute blocks. Multiple things that happen around the same time stack instead of showing as separate competing items.
Why: Caregivers described feeling paralyzed when looking at long to-do lists. Grouping by time window reduces perceived complexity without hiding information.
Shipped
Cautions feature
Persistent, always-visible alerts pinned to the top of the daily view. "Latex allergy." "Do not tell Dad it's medicine."
Why: The cost of missing one is high and the cost of showing it is low. A caregiver should never need to hunt for critical info.
Shipped
Structured but not rigid
Categories (Health, Personal Care, Meal, Leisure) with structured fields, but every activity supports free-text notes.
Why: Caregivers needed structure to make information findable, but flexibility to capture the weird, specific, human details that don't fit in dropdowns.

What we shipped
Reflection
What worked
  • Killed a validated prototype when the research said to. We had expert consensus and a working education app — walking away from that wasn't easy.
  • Centered the patient's care plan, not the caregiver's schedule. This framing decision shaped every feature choice that followed.
  • Made Cautions always visible — not buried in a settings page. The cost of missing one is high; the cost of showing it is low.
What I'd do differently
  • No usage tracking after launch. No retention data, no measurement of whether information transfer improved. Would build basic analytics into the MVP from day one.
  • Cautions feature deserved structured A/B testing. The signal was qualitative, not quantitative — fine for an MVP, but an honest gap.
  • Small usability sample (3–4 caregivers). The validation story would be stronger with a larger pool and longitudinal follow-up.
