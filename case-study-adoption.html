Context

Aegon is a $22.4B insurance group running six customer-facing platforms — consumer website, mobile app, advisor portal, and three internal tools. Each had been built independently. No shared components, no consistent token language, no documentation.

When I joined the design systems team, the component audit revealed 47 distinct button variants across the product suite. The problem wasn't that teams made bad decisions — it's that nobody had given them a shared starting point.

My brief was to take an existing but under-adopted system and turn it into something teams would actually use. That meant the work wasn't just design — it was architecture, documentation, and change management in equal parts.

I owned the audit, redesigned the component architecture with conditional logic instead of platform forks, wrote all documentation myself, and built the adoption pipeline that brought the first three teams onboard within [X months]. Skip to the full process walkthrough ↓

Reflection
What worked
The component brief template
Forcing teams to articulate requirements before design started eliminated most "we need something custom" requests. Structure before solutions.
Writing docs myself
The 89% adoption team said documentation was the reason they could move 4× faster. Delegating this would have been a mistake.
Framing in business terms
Load time reduction as retention. Consistency as reduced support costs. That framing kept the team funded across two org changes.
What I'd do differently
Brief template before the audit
The first audit round had no structured format. Brief first, audit second would have saved [X weeks] of rework when requirements shifted mid-process.
Measure adoption from day one
We started tracking [X months] in. The 89% number would be considerably stronger with a week-one baseline to show trajectory, not just endpoint.
